The Song Beneath the Veil

Dusk settles over Karvossa, the coastal village. The sea hums beneath a wooden stage, its groans timed with the tide, as if the ocean itself leans in to listen. Lanterns sway gently in the salty breeze. A festival is underway—one of music, moonlight, and the slow turning of seasons.

A hush begins to spread as Marcel steps onto the stage.

He moves with practiced ease, his sling at his hip and a bolo wrapped loosely at his belt. A sly grin curves across his lips as he surveys the meandering crowd. He strums a single note, rich and resonant, and the noise of the village stills. Heads turn. Feet pause.

The bard begins to play.

One by one, the villagers gather nearby. Their steps sync to the rhythm. A few sway gently, caught in the current of his music.

“Who in the gods’ names is that?” murmurs a fisherman.

“Marcel of the Moon, I think. Saw him once in Willow Woods,” says another.

“From there, is he?”

“Hard to say. Doesn’t look Vaelorian… or from anywhere I know.”

As the melody shifts, so does the crowd. The notes grow darker, more reflective. Joy fades to wistfulness. Faces soften. A mother clutches her child a little tighter. An old man wipes his eye.

Then, as if sensing their descent into sorrow, Marcel plants his feet and raises a fist high. He bellows a local tune—raw, proud, and utterly Karvossian.

“May we forever be, as Thraxians by the sea;
Finders of what we seek and punishers of the meek!”

The crowd erupts. Fists rise in unison. The villagers join in, their voices crashing like waves. Marcel lets them carry the chorus, stepping back, a performer now masked in jubilation.

But beneath his smile, he feels nothing.

Their cheers wash over me like waves on a cliff, he thinks. I bow, but the applause never touches the hollow place inside me.
---

Later, near the docks, as torches burn low and waves lap against the pilings, Marcel hears a faint tune—a haunting melody, too familiar.

He freezes.

An elderly woman hums by the edge of the pier, her eyes fixed on the moonlit sea.

“Where did you learn that?” Marcel’s voice is calm, but his fingers twitch at his side.

She turns to him slowly. “My mother sang it,” she replies. “Said it belonged to the child-snatchers… to the silver-eyed witch who danced beneath the moon.” Her voice cracks. “You look like her.”

Marcel stiffens.

Could she know? Could she be the link I’ve been searching for?

He steps forward, but she stumbles backward—fear flashing in her eyes. Before he can speak again, she vanishes into the crowd, the last note of her song hanging in the air.

Marcel scans the square, pupils widening. His changeling eyes pierce the low light—but she’s gone.
---

That night, he dreams.

A still black lake stretches before him, moonlight painting the surface in fractured silver. On the far shore, a shadow stands tall, humming the same melody. Her silhouette shimmers. She turns—her eyes gleam like polished silver.

Marcel wakes with a start, breath shallow, heart pounding. The melody echoes in his skull.

He picks up his guitar, fingers moving before he can think. The strings sing.

The strings know it. My fingers know it. But no words. Only melody. I’ve never heard it. Have I?

Magic sparks. White wisps curl from the strings, thickening into golden threads that bind and braid through the air, wrapping around him. Not bardic magic. Something older.  More authentic.

Changeling magic.

Candles flicker violently. The sea beyond his window rises, mirroring his breath. Marcel leans forward and whispers, “I am one with the sea. I am one with the flames.  Sole m wè—help me to see.”

His head reels. Vision fractures. A foggy moor rushes into view. A woman holds an infant to her chest—face blurred, lullaby blending with his own. Then a stone—massive, ancient—etched in runes and carved with a crescent moon. Seven feet tall, four wide. How had he missed it before?

He reaches for it—

Knock. Knock.

He jolts upright, the vision vanishing abruptly as consciousness forces itself upon him.

Still dazed, Marcel opens the door and is greeted by empty space.  Unamused by the disturbance to his vision, he looks about and sees a ragged piece of parchment at his feet.

Make way to the monastery near Vaelport.
Join the monk preparing for an adventure.
Your People will find you.

---

Marcel approaches the monastery and hails the first monk he sees, “Humble and Honorable Monk, who among you is seeking an adventure beyond these walls?”

Seemingly amused at the question, he asks of Marcel, “What manner of monks would we be, to refuse to tend our flock?”  He smiles broadly.  “You seek Jin, one of our finest warrior monks.  If you hurry, you may catch him in the town square preparing to leave with a group for Everthrone.

“May many blessings be upon you for your help!” calls out Marcel as he hastens his pace.

The town square of Vaelport bustles in the early morning light. Merchants arranging their wares with the reverence of priests; travelers muttering to beasts of burden still blinking off dreams. A small gathering formed near the old well, where a tall figure in simple gray robes tightened the bindings on his staff.

Jin.

He moved with quiet purpose, his shaved head glinting with sweat, his shoulders broad and calm as river stones. His eyes were sharp with the kind of clarity that came from years of watching the world without needing to speak about it.

Marcel spotted him immediately. Something—perhaps the stillness in the monk’s breath, or the way he treated his gear like sacred instruments—struck a chord in him.

“Jin?” Marcel called, slowing as he approached.

The monk turned, studying him cautiously. “I am.”

Marcel gave a dramatic bow. “Marcel of the Moon. Bard. Wanderer. Accidental prophet, depending on who you ask.”

Jin raised a brow. “And who is asking?”

“Fate, I think,” Marcel said with a lopsided grin, “or perhaps some very pushy parchment left on my doorstep.”

He offered the note.

Jin read it silently, then looked back at Marcel with new interest. “You may be of some use to us.  Tell me: how are you in a fight?”

“I’d rather sing than fling my sling, but I can certainly make my enemies sting!”  Seeing that his attempt at humor went unrequited he shifted his posture.  “I can hold my own,” he muttered quickly.

“You are a Bard, yet you carry weapons,” Jin said, glancing at the bolo and sling affixed to Marcel’s hip.

“I strive to carry a tune first,” Marcel replied, “but the sling sings too, if needed.”

“Hmmm… You're not what I expected in a Bard,” Jin admitted.

“And you,” Marcel grinned, “are exactly what I didn’t know I needed.”

Jin laughed—low and real—and Marcel, for the first time in weeks, felt something stir in the hollow place beneath his ribs.

Not a song.

A friendship.
